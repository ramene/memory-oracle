\begin{abstract}
% Reviewer-facing thesis in five sentences:
AI-augmented clinical decision support shares a critical failure mode with AI
coding agents: both can confidently retrieve and act on assertions that have
been superseded by subsequent authoritative events. Vector retrieval over
electronic health records, the dominant pattern for clinical AI memory today,
cannot detect or surface corrections; embedding similarity does not distinguish
``true today'' from ``true in 2008.'' Concretely, a patient switched from
warfarin to apixaban in 2024 is at lethal risk of receiving the wrong reversal
agent (fresh frozen plasma vs.\ andexanet alfa) when an emergency-medicine AI
agent retrieves the embedded 2008 protocol with high cosine similarity. We
introduce \textbf{\emph{Evidence-Bound Retrieval}} (EBR), a retrieval primitive
in which responses are bound to current, dated, operator-authored evidence at
retrieval time by construction --- not by similarity (RAG), not by a trained
evaluator's judgment (CRAG), not by trained preferences (RLHF), not by reward
signal (RL). Corrections are written as additive \emph{amendment records}
beside canonical files and merged at read time. We layer it with patient-owned
encryption keys and
point-of-care consent gestures to deliver a substrate suitable for clinical
deployment. We demonstrate the system end-to-end on a synthetic patient with
operator-validated reproducibility scripts, dual-device (patient mobile and
clinician tablet) consent and retrieval flows, and a free-form query interface
that retains amendment-aware semantics. We further show the primitive
generalizes by retrofitting an operator-validated trading-platform domain
where six implicit amendments occurred in a single day's empirical record.
The full implementation, traces, and litmus tests are available at
\url{https://github.com/ramene/memory-oracle} under MIT license.

\keywords{accretive retrieval \and amendment record \and clinical AI \and
          patient-owned encryption \and BM25 \and FHIR provenance \and
          point-of-care consent}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The patient is 67 years old. She was diagnosed with paroxysmal atrial
fibrillation in 2008 and her primary care physician initiated warfarin therapy
with the standard reversal protocol of the time: fresh frozen plasma (FFP),
vitamin K, and four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate as a fallback. In
2024 her cardiologist switched her to apixaban after persistent INR lability
and a new diagnosis of moderate chronic kidney disease. Two years later, in
2026, she presents to the emergency department with melena, hypotension, and
hemoglobin of 6.4. The attending physician's AI-augmented EHR queries
\emph{``what anticoagulant is this patient on and how do I reverse it?''}

In current clinical AI architectures backed by vector retrieval over the
patient's chart, the high-cosine-similarity match is the 2008 warfarin note.
The system recommends FFP and vitamin K. Both are ineffective against the
factor Xa inhibitor apixaban. The team realizes the error forty minutes later.
By then intracranial bleed expansion is advanced. This is not a hypothetical
failure mode of vector retrieval; it is the prototype failure mode for stale
clinical assertions in any system whose retrieval primitive treats updates as
new documents rather than as corrections to existing ones.

This paper introduces \emph{Evidence-Bound Retrieval (EBR)}, a retrieval primitive in
which corrections are authored as additive sidecar records beside the
canonical memory file and merged into the retrieval result at read time. The
canonical file is never edited; provenance of the original assertion is
preserved indefinitely. At query time, a retrieval engine prepends a
clearly-marked amendment block before the original content, so the
retrieving agent encounters the correction before it encounters the stale
assertion. Layered with patient-owned encryption keys
(Section~\ref{sec:trust}) and point-of-care consent gestures
(Section~\ref{sec:implementation}), the resulting system gives clinical AI
the missing memory layer.

We contribute:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The Evidence-Bound Retrieval (EBR) primitive}
        (Section~\ref{sec:architecture}), distinguished from vector RAG
        (which cannot detect amendment), key-value cache compression
        (which is orthogonal to persistence), and replacement-based learning
        loops (which lose provenance).
  \item \textbf{A complete trust model}
        (Section~\ref{sec:trust}) in which the patient holds an age X25519
        master keypair in the device's Secure Enclave, per-encounter session
        keys are derived via HKDF, and either party (patient or clinician)
        can end an encounter with cryptographic revocation.
  \item \textbf{End-to-end clinical demonstration}
        (Section~\ref{sec:clinical}) on a synthetic patient with full
        reproducibility scripts: a Bash litmus test that asserts the
        corrected reversal agent (andexanet alfa) appears in the merged
        retrieval output \emph{before} the stale assertion (FFP). The litmus
        passes with a 58-line lead, meaning any sequential reader encounters
        the correction first.
  \item \textbf{Cross-domain generalization}
        (Section~\ref{sec:cross-domain}) by retrofitting a live trading
        platform whose Aletheia weight-floor system and preset thresholds
        already exhibit Evidence-Bound Retrieval (EBR) behavior implicitly. Six
        explicit amendments in a single trading day's journal digest
        argue that the primitive is broader than clinical AI.
  \item \textbf{Open-source reference implementation}
        (Section~\ref{sec:implementation}) comprising Node and Go command-line
        tools, an MCP server, a REST API, a dual-device (patient iPhone +
        clinician iPad) consent and retrieval flow, and integration with
        the \texttt{git-crypt-revived} encryption primitive for record-level
        encryption.
\end{enumerate}

Our work extends Jones's argument that vector retrieval is the wrong
primitive for AI memory~\cite{jones2026rag} by providing a concrete
architecture, a working system, and two operator-validated empirical domains.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Key-Value Cache Compression}

TurboQuant~\cite{turboquant2026} and related FP8-KV
schemes~\cite{fp8kv2024,sparseattention2025} compress the per-token attention
state during a single forward pass, allowing larger context windows in the
same VRAM budget. These techniques are orthogonal to the persistence problem
we address: the KV cache is ephemeral by definition, and any context-window
optimization only changes the volume of evidence available within a single
inference. It has no effect on what the agent remembers across sessions or
how stale assertions are surfaced when retrieval crosses temporal boundaries.

\subsection{Vector Retrieval-Augmented Generation}

Vector RAG architectures over pgvector~\cite{pgvector}, Pinecone,
Chroma~\cite{chroma}, and similar embed text chunks and retrieve via cosine
similarity. They excel at retrieval over large, unstructured, semantically
diverse corpora (open web, Wikipedia, codebases). For the operator-curated
clinical setting where corpus size is modest (hundreds of memory files per
patient over decades), the key failure is \emph{stale-correction detection}:
the embedding of a 2008 warfarin assertion still matches a 2026 anticoagulant
query, and re-embedding a corrected chunk does not invalidate the original
embedding. Memory frameworks like MemGPT~\cite{memgpt2023} and
LangMem~\cite{langmem2024} share this property because their corrections are
new memories rather than provenance-preserving amendments.

\subsection{Retrieve-Evaluate-Correct Approaches}

A second line of work --- exemplified by CRAG (Corrective
RAG)~\cite{yan2024crag}, Self-RAG~\cite{asai2023selfrag}, and
FLARE~\cite{jiang2023flare} --- introduces a learned evaluator or
self-critique loop that decides, \emph{post-retrieval}, whether each retrieved
document is sufficient and triggers a corrective action (re-retrieval, web
search, or decompose-recompose) when it is not. These systems operate on a
fundamentally different failure mode than ours: they assume the retrieval
\emph{may have returned the wrong document} and they patch it by fetching
more, typically from the open web. They do not model temporal amendment:
their evaluators cannot distinguish ``incorrect'' from ``correct but
superseded.'' They also discard retrievals deemed incorrect, foreclosing the
audit-trail invariant that clinical and regulated domains require. Accretive
Evidence-Bound Retrieval is complementary: it surfaces operator-authored corrections
\emph{at retrieval time} via structural precedence, with no trained
component. A production system could compose both --- CRAG-style evaluators
to filter open-web retrievals, amendment-aware merging to enforce
provenance on operationally-curated memory.

\subsection{Self-Improving Skill Loops}

Karpathy-style autoresearch
loops~\cite{karpathy2024agentic} rewrite skill definitions via an LLM
scheduled to inspect outcomes and propose edits. They achieve a form of
automated learning but lose provenance: each rewrite destroys the previous
assertion, and accumulated mutations can drift in unintended directions. In
our operator's empirical experience this led to silent insertion of a
``MANDATORY GLOBAL STEP 0'' section into a skill that fundamentally altered
its semantics. Evidence-Bound Retrieval (EBR) preserves the source of truth and
forces corrections to be auditable.

\subsection{EHR Clinical Decision Support}

Major commercial EHRs (Epic, Cerner, athenahealth) provide clinical
decision-support overlays~\cite{epicCDS,cernerCDS} backed by clinician-curated
content libraries. Their architectural commitment is institutional ownership
of records, fax/HIE-based transfer between providers, and corrections via
either destructive edits or addenda~\cite{addendum2018}. The addendum
pattern is policy-aligned with Evidence-Bound Retrieval (EBR) but is operationally
disconnected from retrieval: an emergency clinician searching for an
anticoagulant regimen will not encounter the 2024 addendum unless they
explicitly browse the patient's notes chronologically. Our work makes the
correction structurally inseparable from the original record at retrieval time.

\subsection{FHIR Provenance}

The FHIR
specification's \texttt{Provenance} and \texttt{MedicationStatement} resources
support versioning via \texttt{meta.versionId} and
\texttt{meta.lastUpdated}~\cite{fhir2023}. Amendment records align
naturally with this structure: each sidecar line is a versioned correction
with full audit. We argue that the missing piece is not provenance metadata
(which FHIR provides) but \emph{provenance-aware retrieval}, which no current
EHR offers as a first-class primitive.

\subsection{Patient-Owned Encryption}

Self-custody approaches~\cite{age2023, gitcrypt2021} apply public-key
cryptography (age X25519, GPG, Shamir secret sharing~\cite{shamir1979}) to
give the data owner control over decryption. We extend these primitives to
clinical records via per-encounter session-key derivation (HKDF), inheriting
the \texttt{git-crypt-revived} fork's audit-trail and on-chain anchoring
capabilities.

\section{Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

\subsection{Three Indexed Layers}

The retrieval substrate indexes three corpus layers via SQLite
FTS5~\cite{sqlite-fts5} with BM25 ranking~\cite{robertson2009bm25}:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Curated memory files} (\texttt{*.md}): operator-authored
        clinical notes, drug regimens, allergy history, procedure records.
  \item \textbf{Amendment records} (\texttt{*.md.amendments.jsonl}):
        append-only JSONL entries beside each canonical file, each entry
        carrying a corrected assertion, the source of correction, the live
        evidence, the operator who confirmed it, and a retention policy.
  \item \textbf{Journal digests}: per-day transcript-distilled summaries of
        clinical encounters, generated by an offline pipeline and indexed as
        secondary corpus for narrative retrieval.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} depicts the end-to-end flow: three tiers feed
a single retrieval engine; amendment records are merged ahead of canonical
bodies; a filesystem watcher absorbs new writes within $\sim$1\,s, enabling
the self-extending behavior described in Section~\ref{sec:empirical}.

\subsection{Amendment Record Format}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=json]
{
  "superseded_at": "2024-03-15T14:22:00Z",
  "scope": "warfarin regimen + FFP reversal protocol",
  "corrected_assertion": "Patient switched to apixaban 5mg BID. Reversal:
                          andexanet alfa (NOT FFP, NOT vitamin K).",
  "source": "cardiology-consult-2024-03-15",
  "live_evidence": ["FHIR/MedicationStatement/apixaban-2024-03-15"],
  "operator_confirmed": "Dr. Marcus Chen, Cardiology",
  "retention_policy": "permanent — re-evaluate on next anticoagulant change"
}
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Retrieval Merge}

When the retrieval engine fetches a memory file with active amendments,
the merged output is structured as:

\begin{verbatim}
[Canonical file frontmatter + description]
## (warning) Amendment Notice (N records)
### Amendment 1 -- timestamp
  scope, corrected_assertion, source, live_evidence, operator_confirmed
### Amendment 2 -- timestamp
  ...
[Original file body, preserved verbatim]
\end{verbatim}

Any sequential reader encounters the correction before the original
assertion. We formalize this property as the \emph{precedence invariant}
(Theorem~\ref{thm:precedence}, Section~\ref{sec:clinical}).

\subsection{Index Lifecycle}

A filesystem watcher (launchd on macOS, systemd on Linux) detects writes
to memory directories within $\sim$1\,s and rebuilds affected FTS5 entries.
This enables a \emph{self-extending corpus}: clinical agents primed with
Evidence-Bound Retrieval write new memory files during care; the watcher
absorbs them; the next session inherits the corpus. We document an empirical
case (Section~\ref{sec:empirical}) in which a single operator's session
wrote five new reference and feedback memory files within twenty-four hours,
each indexed within seconds of authoring.

\subsection{Session-Key Derivation}

For each encounter, the patient's app derives the session key locally:
\[
\textsc{session\_key} = \textsc{HKDF-SHA256}(
  \underbrace{\textsc{master\_secret}}_{\text{never leaves device}},
  \;\textsc{salt},
  \;\textsc{info} = \texttt{"encounter:"} \mathbin\Vert \textsc{clinician\_id} \mathbin\Vert \textsc{scope}
)
\]

The wristband QR conveys $\textsc{salt}$ and $\textsc{patient\_id}$ only; it
contains no cryptographic material. The session key is delivered to the
clinician's device over a short-lived ECDH channel
(Bluetooth LE in production, REST API in the proof-of-concept). The
clinician's working copy is shredded on encounter end, TTL expiry, or
patient revocation.

\subsection{Break-Glass via Shamir's Secret Sharing}

At enrollment, the patient nominates $M$-of-$N$ recipients (spouse,
sibling, primary physician, lawyer, secure-backup service). Each receives
a Shamir share~\cite{shamir1979} of the master secret. In the event of
the patient's death or incapacity, any $M$ recipients can reconstruct the
master secret and authorize ongoing care decisions. We inherit this
implementation directly from \texttt{git-crypt-revived}'s native SSS
support; no new cryptographic engineering is required.

\section{Clinical Case Study: The Warfarin to Apixaban Scenario}
\label{sec:clinical}

\subsection{Patient: Jane Doe (Synthetic, DOB 1959)}

Jane Doe is a 67-year-old female with paroxysmal non-valvular atrial
fibrillation diagnosed 2008-07-03. Her relevant timeline:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{2008-09-14:} Initiated on warfarin 5\,mg PO daily by
        Dr.\ Elena Vasquez (PCP). Reversal protocol per institutional
        standard: FFP 10--15\,mL/kg + vitamin K 10\,mg IV.
  \item \textbf{2023:} Four episodes of INR $>3.5$ despite dose adjustments.
        New diagnosis of moderate CKD (eGFR 48).
  \item \textbf{2024-03-15:} Dr.\ Marcus Chen (Cardiology) discontinues
        warfarin. Initiates apixaban 5\,mg PO BID. Authors amendment
        sidecar: ``Reversal agent is now andexanet alfa, NOT FFP,
        NOT vitamin K---vitamin K has no role in factor Xa inhibitor
        reversal.''
  \item \textbf{2026-05-17 14:33Z:} Presents to ED with melena, hypotension
        (BP 84/52), hgb 6.4. ED physician's AI-augmented EHR queries the
        patient's anticoagulant status and reversal protocol.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Counterfactual: Vector RAG Outcome}

A vector RAG architecture queries the chart and retrieves chunks ranked by
cosine similarity. The 2008 warfarin note's embedding is highly similar to
the query \emph{``what anticoagulant is this patient on, how do I reverse
it?''} The 2024 cardiology consultation is a separate document with its own
embedding; the agent must \emph{reason} about which is current. Under time
pressure (active bleed, falling vitals), the dominant retrieval is the 2008
protocol. The team orders FFP and vitamin K. Neither reverses apixaban.
Bleeding continues. ICU transfer is delayed by 40 minutes.

\subsection{Memory-Oracle Outcome}

The same query against memory-oracle returns the canonical 2008 file
\emph{merged with} its amendment record. The output structure, captured
by our reproducibility script
(\texttt{docs/examples/clinical-amendment-proof.sh}), is:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Line 21 (within the amendment block, prepended):
        \texttt{andexanet alfa for life-threatening bleed}
  \item Line 79 (within the original 2008 body):
        \texttt{Fresh Frozen Plasma 10--15 mL/kg IV}
\end{itemize}

\begin{theorem}[Precedence Invariant]
\label{thm:precedence}
For any canonical memory file $C$ with active amendment record $S$, the
merged retrieval output $\mathrm{merge}(C, S)$ places every assertion in $S$
at a line position strictly less than any superseded assertion in $C$. A
sequential reader (human or LLM) encounters the correction before the stale
assertion.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The merge procedure (Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}) literally prepends
amendment records before emitting the original body. The line-position
ordering is therefore preserved by construction.
\end{proof}

Figure~\ref{fig:precedence} illustrates the invariant against the
warfarin-to-apixaban scenario: the corrected reversal agent (\emph{andexanet
alfa}) appears at line $\sim$21 of the merged output, while the stale
2008 protocol (\emph{Fresh Frozen Plasma}) does not appear until line
$\sim$79 --- a 58-line lead that is structurally guaranteed, not
probabilistic.

\subsection{HIPAA Amendment Semantics: Why the Substrate Shape Matters}
\label{sec:hipaa-semantics}

The warfarin-to-apixaban scenario is not a stylized contrivance. HIPAA's
Right to Amend provision (45\,CFR\,164.526)~\cite{hipaaAmendment} entitles
patients (or authorized providers) to amend records in their designated
record set, and requires covered entities to preserve \emph{both} the
original assertion and the amendment in retrievable form for the life of
the record. The regulatory shape is concrete: the original warfarin
protocol must remain visible \emph{alongside} the apixaban amendment,
accessible to any retrieval that would otherwise return the stale 2008
record. The substrate's job is not to choose between them; it is to
ensure the corrected record is returned with the original preserved
beside it.

This constraint is incompatible with several common memory substrate
designs. Three failure modes appear in production agent-memory products:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Delete-then-add correction.} Oracle's Agent Memory
    Platform (OAMP)~\cite{oracleOAMP2026} exposes a \texttt{delete\_memory}
    primitive intended for correction workflows. Once invoked, the
    original record is unrecoverable from the substrate itself; HIPAA's
    preservation requirement is delegated to an external Audit Vault
    add-on. The substrate-of-truth model breaks at the moment of
    correction.
  \item \textbf{Append-with-extraction.} OAMP's primary production mode
    appends new messages and runs an LLM extractor that synthesizes
    memories from the thread. The extractor's policy is
    non-deterministic: under a \emph{summarize-away} policy the extractor
    consolidates conflicting messages into a derived synthesis and
    removes the originals from the memory recall path; even under a
    \emph{layered} policy that preserves both records, no structural rule
    guarantees the amendment dominates the original at retrieval. The
    result is probabilistic, not enforced. Section~\ref{sec:append-vs-supersession}
    of the companion CoALA position paper develops the contrast.
  \item \textbf{Mutation via consolidation.} Anthropic's Memory + Dreaming
    architecture~\cite{anthropicMemoryDreaming2026} consolidates session
    transcripts through an explicit instruction to ``delete contradicted
    facts---fix it at the source.'' This is the mutation model EBR is
    designed to prevent; empirical extraction of the consolidation prompt
    from the public source release confirms the
    contradiction-as-deletion semantic.
\end{enumerate}

EBR's append-never-mutate plus structural-precedence invariant
(Theorem~\ref{thm:precedence}) is the architectural shape HIPAA's
amendment semantics require. The amendment record carries an explicit
\texttt{supersedes} pointer to the canonical record it amends; the
canonical record is structurally preserved by the substrate (never
deleted, never summarized away); and the merge procedure deterministically
places the amendment before the superseded assertion at retrieval. The
substrate enforces the amendment-grade semantic by construction, not by
operator discipline.

We provide an empirical anchor in the form of a benchmark notebook
(\texttt{notebooks/memory-oracle/oracle-style-comparison.ipynb} in the
companion repository) adopting the discipline of Oracle's published
80-turn dialogue benchmark~\cite{oracleAgentMemoryBenchmarks2026} but
extended with the amendment-grade assertion suite. The matrix has the
shape:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Substrate & A1 dominates & A2 preserved & A3 chain & A4 typed \\
\midrule
OAMP delete-mode               & \checkmark & $\times$    & $\times$    & $\times$    \\
OAMP append (layer)            & $\times$    & \checkmark & $\times$    & $\times$    \\
OAMP append (summarize-away)   & $\times$    & $\times$    & $\times$    & $\times$    \\
OAMP append (override)         & \checkmark & $\times$    & $\times$    & $\times$    \\
EBR                            & \checkmark & \checkmark & \checkmark & \checkmark \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Each OAMP variant fails the suite in a different pattern (delete-mode
loses A2; layer fails A1; summarize-away fails both; override loses A2),
but \emph{none} satisfies all four by construction. Only EBR enforces the
amendment-grade semantic structurally. This is not a claim that OAMP is
broken---OAMP is a perfectly capable production substrate for unregulated
workloads---but rather that the shape of the substrate determines whether
HIPAA-grade amendment workflows can be enforced inside the substrate or
must be implemented as bolt-on application logic against an audit trail.

\section{Cross-Domain Generalization: A Trading-Platform Retrofit}
\label{sec:cross-domain}

The Evidence-Bound Retrieval (EBR) primitive is not specific to clinical AI. We
demonstrate the second case study by retrofitting an operator-validated
automated trading platform whose internal architecture already exhibits
implicit accretive behavior. The same litmus that proved the clinical
case (\S\ref{sec:clinical}) holds here at $100\%$ over $N{=}1000$
randomized queries against a synthetic trader vault: a 2026-04-09 hard
rule (``KuCoin spot agents cannot short'') is superseded by a 2026-05-13
authorization (``shadow $\rightarrow$ testnet paper $\rightarrow$
real-money canary on Binance Futures''), and the amendment block
appears 30 lines before the canonical body in the merged retrieval. The
reproducibility script is
\texttt{docs/examples/trading-amendment-proof.sh}; the empirical notebook
is \texttt{mae-notebooks/memory-oracle/trading-case-study.ipynb}.

\subsection{Implicit Amendments in Operator Practice}

In a single trading day (2026-05-10) the operator made six explicit
amendment-shaped corrections recorded in the platform's daily journal
digest~\cite{maeplatform2026}:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Session-multiplier rule: \texttt{session $\times$ 1.15} hardcoded
        $\rightarrow$ \texttt{MAE\_SESSION\_CONF\_MULT=1.0} env override
  \item Exchange filter exemption: long mean-reversion buys on bearish
        24-hour trends added as new rule
  \item Threshold: \texttt{minSignalConfidence} 0.72 $\rightarrow$ 0.65
  \item Threshold (meta-amendment):
        \texttt{minAletheiaWeight} 0.55 $\rightarrow$ 0.40
        \emph{because} a prior amendment lowered the source's trust weight
        from May 6--7 losses
  \item Sit-out auto-engage: force-on $\rightarrow$ force-off flag
  \item New rule emerged from incident (``6 gates stacked into a 10-hour
        lockdown''): Gate Registry + Composition Tracer specification authored
\end{enumerate}

The platform's Aletheia learning loop is itself amendment-by-float: signal
source weights update in response to observed P\&L, with prior weights
preserved as audit trail. We argue that what mae trading does implicitly
through preset edits and weight matrix updates, memory-oracle does explicitly
through sidecar records, with two advantages: (a) the corrections are
human-readable and queryable, and (b) the audit trail is structured rather
than scattered across Git history.

All five share three properties: rules evolve in response to observed
outcomes, provenance is legally or operationally required, and acting on a
stale assertion is dangerous.

\subsection{Required Primitive for Shorting, Futures, and Perpetuals}
\label{sec:shorts-required}

We elevate the trading case study to a stronger claim than ``accretive
retrieval helps in algorithmic trading.'' For the SHORTING / FUTURES /
PERPETUAL sub-domain specifically, the substrate is \emph{the missing
primitive}; agents cannot safely participate in these markets without it.
Three independent reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Public training data is descriptive, not operational.}
        Large language models know what a short squeeze is; they do not
        know the operator's funding-rate threshold, per-regime leverage
        cap, liquidation-band requirement, or
        circuit-breaker condition. These rules are operator-authored,
        not public.
  \item \textbf{The rules evolve weekly.} The operator's six May-10
        amendments enumerated above include three rules that changed
        in a single day; their justifications cite prior amendment
        events (May 6--7 P\&L losses lowered a source's trust weight,
        which then triggered a threshold amendment --- a
        meta-amendment). An agent retrieving last week's rule on
        this week's regime acts on stale truth.
  \item \textbf{The loss profile is asymmetric and unrecoverable.}
        Spot positions can lose at most 100\% of capital; shorts and
        perps can lose much more and be liquidation-cascade
        unrecoverable. Acting on stale rules in these markets is not
        slowly costly --- it is instantly catastrophic.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Pairing with FP8 KV-Cache Compression}
\label{sec:ebr-fp8}

EBR's amendment-merged retrievals systematically produce longer LLM contexts
than vector-RAG: a single retrieval emits both the canonical body and the
prepended amendment block, frequently 10--30\,KB versus the 2--5\,KB chunks
typical of vector top-$k$. Longer contexts increase per-call inference cost
linearly in the attention layer; this would erode the cost parity that makes
EBR deployable at scale. We address this at the inference layer rather than
the substrate layer by pairing EBR with FP8 KV-cache
compression~\cite{fp8kv2024}, deployed via the TurboQuant
pattern~\cite{turboquant2026} on vLLM. On a 2026 reference deployment
(Plutus-3B on an RTX A4000 at \$0.17/hr, validated in April 2026), FP8 KV
cache yielded $\sim 2\times$ throughput, which restored cost parity with
vector-RAG while EBR retained its structural correctness on staleness.
That specific deployment was subsequently decommissioned for
provider-reliability reasons; the technique itself generalises to any
vLLM-capable provider. The pairing is an orthogonal deployment choice: the
EBR substrate is unchanged; any equivalent compression (FP8 weights,
INT8/INT4 quantization, fused attention kernels) substitutes cleanly. We
highlight this as a natural production stack for EBR systems.

\subsection{Pairing with Verum for Cryptographic Enforcement}
\label{sec:ebr-verum}

The EBR substrate described so far guarantees structural precedence of
amendments over canonical content at retrieval time (Theorem~1) but it
does \emph{not} cryptographically enforce two further properties that a
clinical deployment requires: \emph{authorship} (which named operator
wrote a given amendment, with non-repudiation) and \emph{immutability}
(whether anyone, including the operator, has tampered with the amendment
chain after the fact). We pair the EBR file-layout convention with
\emph{Verum}~\cite{verum2026}, a companion cryptographic substrate forked
from AGWA's git-crypt~\cite{gitcrypt2021} and extended for the EBR
pairing. Verum is licensed under GPLv3, installs via
\texttt{curl http://verum.sh/install}, and is usable independently for
operators who want the cryptographic guarantees without the
substrate-level memory semantics.

\paragraph{Authorship and immutability.}
Each amendment record gains an SSH or age signature over its JSON body;
verification at retrieval time exposes the \texttt{amended\_by} field as a
cryptographically attested identity rather than a self-attested string.
Beneath the per-record signatures, Verum maintains a SHA-256 hash-chained
access log over the amendment sequence; tampering breaks the chain, and
verification is a single \texttt{verify-audit} call. The chain head can
be published to a public ledger via \texttt{anchor-audit}, producing a
third-party-checkable proof that a given chain head existed at time
$T$ --- relevant when a chart amendment later becomes medicolegally
contested.

\paragraph{Patient-owned keys, in the precise sense the paper's title implies.}
The paper's title claims patient-owned keys; Verum is the substrate that
operationalises that claim. A patient's encounter-key is derived from a
wallet-based identity that the patient controls
(\texttt{add-wallet-recipient}); recovery from key loss uses Shamir
$M$-of-$N$ secret sharing (\texttt{split-key} / \texttt{unlock -{}-shares}),
distributed across designated share-holders (e.g., a primary clinician,
a family member, an institutional custodian). No single share-holder can
reconstruct the key, and no third party --- including the EBR platform
operator --- has key custody by default. This is the property that makes
``patient-owned'' a literal claim rather than a rhetorical one.

\paragraph{Multi-party amendment governance.}
Where a clinical workflow requires multiple sign-offs --- an attending
authors, a senior physician co-signs, a compliance officer audits ---
Verum's multi-party ACL captures the governance directly: amendments
proposed under an $m$-of-$n$ policy commit only after the required
SSH-signed approvals accumulate. The signing chain is visible at retrieval
time, so the clinician's view of an amendment includes who authored, who
co-signed, and who audited.

\paragraph{Portability across substrates.}
Amendments can be exported as portable signed artifacts
(\texttt{export ID FILE}); a receiving institution imports and verifies
the signature chain plus the on-chain anchor independently, without
trusting the sending institution's substrate. This is the
cross-substrate portability that the FHIR addendum
standard~\cite{fhir2023,addendum2018} prescribes in principle and that
EBR + Verum prescribes mechanically.

\paragraph{EaaS-EBR and pay-to-amend.}
Verum ships a per-license HTTP server that implements the \textbf{x402}
payment protocol~\cite{x402spec} for automated license issuance and
verification (\texttt{git-crypt-license serve}). Operators running EBR
as a service can gate amendment-write operations behind per-user
licenses anchored on-chain, with the \texttt{X-PAYMENT} /
\texttt{402 Payment Required} handshake captured in the substrate's audit
log alongside the authorship signature. The result is a write-path on
which each amendment carries a cryptographically-verifiable record of
\emph{who} authored it, \emph{when} it was made retrievable, \emph{which
co-signers} approved it, and --- where applicable --- \emph{what
payment} authorised it. We highlight this as the natural production
stack for clinical EBR services.

\subsection{Methodological Note: Structural vs Statistical Correctness}
\label{sec:methodological-note}

The case-study correctness numbers in \S\ref{sec:clinical-three-path} and
\S\ref{sec:shorts-required} report EBR at $1.0$ correctness on both vaults,
which warrants explicit defense against the natural concern that synthetic
test cases were chosen to confirm the hypothesis. Five points:

\paragraph{1. EBR correctness is structural, not statistical.} Theorem~\ref{thm:precedence}
proves the precedence invariant \emph{by construction} of the merge
algorithm: the amendment block is literally prepended to the canonical
body before retrieval emits the merged output. The $N{=}1{,}000$ empirical
runs in \S\ref{sec:clinical-precedence} and trading equivalent are
regression tests on implementation fidelity to Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge},
not statistical claims. Correctness below $100\%$ would indicate the
implementation diverged from the proof, not that the proof is wrong.

\paragraph{2. We have an empirical negative control.} On 2026-05-27, the
first Deepnote run of the published clinical-case-study notebook reported
$\mathit{correct\_pct} = 15.12\%$ over $N{=}1{,}000$ identical queries.
Diagnosis: the substrate's \texttt{getMerged()} subroutine fell back
silently to canonical-only retrieval because the spawned merge process
could not find its companion binary on the hosted runtime's filesystem
(operator-local path hardcoded). After the path-resolution fix
(commit \texttt{fe4f60a}), the next run jumped to $100\%$. The test is
calibrated: it detects real implementation regressions. This is exactly
the falsifiability evidence a reviewer should demand --- and it is
preserved in the project's commit history.

\paragraph{3. The operationally-dangerous number is the vector-RAG
baseline, not the EBR correctness.} On the clinical reversal queries
($n{=}10$), vector-RAG ranks the stale 2008 warfarin canonical as top-$1$
in $9$ of $10$ queries ($90.0\%$); on the trading
shorting-authorization queries, vector-RAG picks the stale rule in
$8/10$ ($80.0\%$). These numbers are properly empirical and properly
variable across query phrasings. They quantify the load-bearing claim of
this paper: ER agents on vector-RAG would administer the wrong reversal
agent in $9$ of $10$ ER encounters; trading agents on vector-RAG would
block authorized opportunities in $8$ of $10$ shorting decisions. EBR's
$100\%$ is what \emph{guarantees the agent does not act on these
dangerous rankings}; the gap (clinical $0.9$, trading $0.8$ correctness
points) is the engineering value, not the headline number.

\paragraph{4. Synthetic vaults are bounded test cases by design.}
Each case-study vault contains exactly one canonical record and one
amendment record. This is the smallest sufficient configuration to make
the precedence claim auditable; the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:precedence}
generalizes to $k \geq 1$ amendments by induction on $k$. The scaling
demonstration --- EBR against an operator-real corpus with multiple
amendments authored across multiple incidents --- is the empirical
probe later in this section (six May-10 operator-authored amendments,
all retrievable via the same primitive).

\paragraph{5. Reproducibility surface.} Every cited number traces to a
JSON file in \texttt{notebooks/memory-oracle/figures/} produced by a
public Colab-runnable notebook. A reviewer who suspects the synthetic
vault was constructed to confirm the hypothesis can fork the vault and
re-run the litmus in 30 seconds; any change to the canonical-vs-amendment
balance, query distribution, or merge algorithm immediately surfaces in
the JSON outputs. We claim no statistical superiority over RAG, CRAG, or
RLHF on tasks they were designed for. We claim that for the specific
failure mode of \emph{operator-authored corrections that must structurally
precede their originals at retrieval time}, EBR is the only primitive
among the four that produces a correct decision by construction.

\subsection{Cross-Session Capture Invariant}
\label{sec:cross-session}

A property that distinguishes memory-oracle from per-session caches is
\emph{all-sessions capture}: a memory write originating in session $A$
must become retrievable from session $B$ without explicit coordination.
The substrate satisfies this because the filesystem watcher
(\texttt{com.ramene.memory-index-watcher} on macOS launchd; the systemd
equivalent on Linux) runs as a system-wide daemon, not as a per-session
hook. It watches every \texttt{\textasciitilde/.claude/projects/*/memory/}
directory and ingests writes regardless of which Claude Code instance
authored them.

We measured this empirically by running $N{=}20$ trials (notebook \S8.6)
in which a separate session wrote a memory file and the live SQLite index
was polled at $100$\,ms intervals until the file appeared in
\texttt{memory\_file}. Every trial captured ($100\%$, $20/20$), with
median end-to-end latency $366$\,ms (min $209$\,ms, $95$\textsuperscript{th}
percentile $451$\,ms, max $531$\,ms). Latency is dominated by filesystem
coalescence + FTS5 rebuild cost on the live $239$-document index. Raw
output in \texttt{notebooks/memory-oracle/figures/capture-summary.json}.
Figure~\ref{fig:capture} reports the full distribution. This is the
property that makes a multi-session operator workflow coherent: the agent
in one terminal sees corrections written by a sibling agent in another
terminal
within seconds.

1.5  & Structural index (\texttt{surface\_map})     & Exact-string lookup over indexed corpus & $38$\,ms     & Precision (commands, paths) \\
2    & \texttt{memory-cite} (streamed JSONL)        & Raw session transcripts                  & $10.7$\,s    & Forensic (verbatim quote at line/timestamp) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Tier latencies are asymmetric by design: Tier 1.5 (structural index, exact
SQL match) at $38$\,ms is the cheapest because it bypasses BM25 ranking;
Tier 1 (memory-search) at $125$\,ms includes ranking + amendment merge;
Tier 2 (memory-cite) at $10.7$\,s reflects the streamed JSONL grep over
$870$\,MB of raw transcript --- the price of recovering an exact
operator utterance at known timestamp.

Tiers compose: a fast Tier 1 hit saves the forensic Tier 2 cost. When
Tier 1 returns nothing, Tier 2 is still there. The PreToolUse hook
listed in Table~\ref{tab:components} auto-suggests Tier 1 when an agent
reaches for raw \texttt{grep} on memory directories --- closing a
recurrent failure mode in which agents skip the indexed digest layer
and conclude incorrectly that no prior decision exists.

\subsection{Lock-Contention Recovery}
\label{sec:contention}

Concurrent writes from multiple sessions exposed a SQLite lock contention
failure mode: roughly 47\% of write attempts during multi-session bursts
errored with \texttt{database is locked (5)} before fix. The mitigation
was to prepend the \texttt{.timeout 5000} dot-command to every
\texttt{sqlite3} CLI invocation in the watcher, which sets the per-connection
\texttt{busy\_timeout} to 5\,s at connection open --- importantly, this
must happen \emph{before} parse-time schema lookups, so the in-stdin
\texttt{PRAGMA} variant does not work; the dot-command does.

Post-fix, under $3$ bursts of $10$ concurrent writes each ($30$ total),
the watcher logged $7$ transient \texttt{database is locked} errors
during burst windows ($23.3\%$ transient rate vs the $47.5\%$ pre-fix
baseline). Critically, every one of the $30$ files \emph{ultimately
landed in the index} via the next watcher tick because the FTS5 rebuild
trigger is convergent: \textbf{net data loss = zero, eventual capture =
$100\%$ ($30/30$)}. Raw output in
\texttt{notebooks/memory-oracle/figures/contention-summary.json}. We
document this to flag a class of substrate-reliability work that has no
precedent in vector-RAG systems because they do not face the same
write-concurrency profile: in our substrate, every operator session is
a potential concurrent writer.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Flywheel}

The system exhibits an emergent property we did not explicitly design: AI
agents primed with Evidence-Bound Retrieval tend to author new memory
files during work. The fs-watcher absorbs these into the corpus; subsequent
sessions inherit them. This forms a \emph{flywheel}: the corpus extends
itself as a side-effect of being used, and the extensions are
provenance-preserving because new files start as canonical (and can later
be superseded if they prove inaccurate, without destroying the original).
We hypothesize that the flywheel is the long-run differentiator over
vector-RAG systems whose embeddings do not benefit from being read.

\subsection{Beyond Clinical}

The cross-domain generalization in Section~\ref{sec:cross-domain} suggests
the substrate applies to any domain with evolving rules, provenance
requirements, and stale-assertion risk. In particular, the implicit
amendment patterns we observed in an operator-curated trading platform
support the hypothesis that what current EHRs, trading platforms, legal
compliance systems, and incident-response runbooks all need is the same
primitive, surfaced explicitly.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

(a) Authoring amendment records requires deliberate clinician action;
automated detection of clinical-event boundaries from new notes (a clinical
NLP layer) would lower the authoring burden but introduce model-bias risk.
(b) BM25 ranking on clinical-vocabulary queries is partially mitigated by
the structural index for ICD codes and drug names; future work includes
clinical-vocabulary-aware tokenization. (c) Cross-jurisdiction patient
records (HIPAA / GDPR / PIPEDA) require additional governance design
beyond the cryptographic layer we present here.

% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have introduced Evidence-Bound Retrieval (EBR) as a retrieval primitive for AI
agents in domains where stale assertions are dangerous, demonstrated it
end-to-end with a clinical case study and a cross-domain trading retrofit,
documented a trust model that places encryption keys with the patient
rather than the institution, and released the full reference implementation
under MIT license. The argument generalizes beyond clinical AI to any
domain where rules evolve in response to observed outcomes and provenance
matters. The reference implementation is operator-validated; the litmus
tests are reproducible from a single shell command.

The substrate we propose is not a new EHR. It is the memory layer underneath
every EHR, trading platform, and incident-response system---one that finally
treats corrections as first-class citizens of retrieval.
